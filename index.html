  • Incidence: 270-440 per 100,000 population

Sepsis Pathophysiological Cascade

Click on each step to view detailed mechanisms, mediators, and intervention points.

1. Infection Onset & Pathogen Recognition

Initial invasion by bacteria, viruses, fungi, or parasites

Mechanism: Pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) recognized by pattern recognition receptors (PRRs)
Key Receptors: Toll-like receptors (TLR2, TLR4, TLR9), NOD-like receptors
Markers: Early elevation of procalcitonin
Intervention: Early source identification, blood cultures before antibiotics

2. Innate Immune Activation

Rapid mobilization of neutrophils, macrophages, and complement

Mechanism: TLR activation triggers NF-κB signaling pathway
Key Mediators: TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, complement C3a/C5a
Markers: WBC elevation/depression, left shift, toxic granulation
Clinical Signs: Fever/hypothermia, tachycardia, tachypnea

3. Cytokine Storm & Systemic Inflammation

Excessive pro-inflammatory cytokine release (SIRS)

Mechanism: Dysregulated "cytokine storm" with loss of homeostatic control
Key Mediators: TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6, IL-8, IFN-γ, HMGB1
Markers: Elevated CRP, procalcitonin >2 ng/mL, ferritin
Intervention: Early antibiotics (within 1 hour), source control
Phase: Potentially reversible with aggressive treatment

4. Endothelial Dysfunction & Capillary Leak

Vascular barrier breakdown and third-spacing of fluids

Mechanism: Cytokine-induced endothelial activation, increased permeability, loss of glycocalyx
Key Mediators: VEGF, angiopoietin-2, sphingosine-1-phosphate
Markers: Lactate elevation, decreased albumin, edema
Clinical Signs: Hypotension, tissue edema, decreased urine output
Intervention: IV crystalloid resuscitation (30 mL/kg), albumin in select cases

5. Coagulation Cascade Activation

Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) and microthrombi formation

Mechanism: Tissue factor expression, thrombin generation, impaired fibrinolysis, consumption of clotting factors
Key Mediators: Tissue factor, thrombin, PAI-1, protein C depletion
Markers: Thrombocytopenia, prolonged PT/PTT, elevated D-dimer, low fibrinogen
Clinical Signs: Bleeding, purpura, acral cyanosis
Intervention: Treat underlying sepsis, supportive care (transfusion as needed)

6. Organ Dysfunction (Sepsis Definition)

Microvascular failure leading to cellular hypoxia and organ injury

Mechanism: Impaired oxygen delivery/utilization, mitochondrial dysfunction, cellular apoptosis
Diagnostic Criteria: SOFA score increase ≥2 points
Organ Systems: Respiratory (ARDS), Cardiovascular (shock), Renal (AKI), Hepatic (cholestasis), Neurological (encephalopathy), Hematologic (DIC)
Markers: Lactate >2 mmol/L, creatinine elevation, bilirubin elevation, platelet drop
Intervention: Organ support (mechanical ventilation, vasopressors, RRT)

7. Septic Shock

Persistent hypotension requiring vasopressors, lactate >2 despite fluids

Definition: Sepsis + vasopressor requirement (MAP ≥65) + lactate >2 mmol/L after adequate fluid resuscitation
Mechanism: Profound vasodilation, myocardial depression, distributive shock
Markers: Lactate >2-4 mmol/L, ScvO2 abnormalities
Mortality: 40-60% (22.5% if SEP-1 bundle met)
Intervention: Norepinephrine (first-line vasopressor), consider vasopressin/epinephrine, stress-dose steroids if refractory

8. Multiple Organ Failure (MOF)

Irreversible organ damage in 3+ organ systems

Mechanism: Prolonged cellular hypoxia, mitochondrial damage, cellular death
Prognosis: Mortality increases with each additional organ system failure
SOFA Score: Typically >12-15
Phase: Often irreversible despite maximal support
Management: Maximal organ support, palliative care discussions

Biomarker Diagnostic Performance

Clinical Interpretation:
  • Procalcitonin: Most specific for bacterial sepsis; guides antibiotic initiation and de-escalation
  • Lactate: Best marker for tissue hypoperfusion; serial measurements guide resuscitation
  • CRP: Non-specific inflammatory marker; slower to rise and fall than PCT
  • Combine markers for improved diagnostic accuracy rather than relying on single biomarker

SEP-1 Bundle: Time-Critical Interventions

Evidence: Compliance with 3-hour and 6-hour bundles reduces septic shock mortality from 42.2% to 22.5%

T = 0 (Recognition)

Sepsis Identified: Suspected infection + SOFA ≥2 or qSOFA ≥2

Start clock for bundle compliance

T = 0-3 Hours: 3-Hour Bundle
  • Measure lactate level (initial baseline for resuscitation)
  • Obtain blood cultures BEFORE antibiotics (2 sets from different sites)
  • Administer broad-spectrum antibiotics (within 1 hour ideal, 3 hours maximum)
  • Begin IV fluid resuscitation with 30 mL/kg crystalloids if hypotensive or lactate ≥4 mmol/L
Critical: Each hour delay in antibiotic administration increases mortality by 7.6% in septic shock
T = 3-6 Hours: 6-Hour Bundle
  • Re-measure lactate if initially elevated (target >20% reduction or normalization)
  • Reassess volume status and tissue perfusion (capillary refill, skin mottling, mental status)
  • Document reassessment with exam or two of: CVP, ScvO2, bedside echo, passive leg raise
  • Initiate vasopressors if MAP <65 mmHg despite adequate fluid resuscitation
Ongoing Management
  • Source control evaluation (imaging, surgical consultation if indicated)
  • De-escalate antibiotics based on cultures and clinical improvement
  • Reassess fluid status frequently to avoid overload
  • Monitor for complications (ARDS, AKI, DIC)

Empiric Antibiotic Selection by Infection Source

Respiratory Source (Pneumonia)

Low Risk (Community-Acquired)

  • Ceftriaxone 2g IV q24h + Azithromycin 500mg IV daily
  • Alternative: Levofloxacin 750mg IV daily (monotherapy)
  • Covers: S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, atypicals

High Risk (HAP/VAP, Pseudomonas risk)

  • Vancomycin 15-20mg/kg IV q8-12h (MRSA coverage)
  • PLUS Ceftazidime 2g IV q8h or Cefepime 2g IV q8h
  • Add Levofloxacin 750mg IV daily for atypical coverage
  • Covers: MRSA, Pseudomonas, resistant GNRs, atypicals

Gastrointestinal/Intra-Abdominal Source

Low Risk (Mild Severity)

  • Ceftriaxone 2g IV q24h + Metronidazole 500mg IV q8h
  • Alternative: Ertapenem 1g IV daily
  • Covers: E. coli, Klebsiella, anaerobes

High Risk (Severe, Healthcare-Associated)

  • Piperacillin/Tazobactam 4.5g IV q6h (extended infusion)
  • OR Cefepime 2g IV q8h + Metronidazole 500mg IV q8h
  • Consider adding Vancomycin if critically ill
  • Covers: ESBL, Pseudomonas, anaerobes, resistant GNRs

Urinary Tract Source (Pyelonephritis/Urosepsis)

Low Risk (Community-Acquired)

  • Ceftriaxone 2g IV q24h
  • Alternative: Levofloxacin 750mg IV daily
  • Covers: E. coli, Proteus, Klebsiella

High Risk (Recent hospitalization, instrumentation)

  • Cefepime 2g IV q8h or Ceftazidime 2g IV q8h
  • For severe: Imipenem 500mg IV q6h or Meropenem 1g IV q8h
  • Covers: ESBL E. coli, Pseudomonas, Enterococcus

Skin & Soft Tissue Source (Cellulitis/Necrotizing Infection)

Non-Purulent Cellulitis

  • Ceftriaxone 2g IV q24h
  • OR Cefazolin 2g IV q8h
  • Covers: Streptococcus, MSSA

Purulent/Necrotizing or MRSA Risk

  • Vancomycin 15-20mg/kg IV q8-12h (MRSA)
  • PLUS Piperacillin/Tazobactam 4.5g IV q6h (polymicrobial)
  • Add Clindamycin 900mg IV q8h (toxin suppression for necrotizing infections)
  • URGENT SURGICAL CONSULTATION for necrotizing fasciitis
Important Notes:
  • Adjust dosing for renal/hepatic impairment
  • De-escalate based on culture results and clinical improvement (typically 48-72 hours)
  • Consider local antibiogram and resistance patterns
  • Procalcitonin-guided therapy can help determine duration

Fluid Resuscitation Calculator

SEP-1 Requirement: 30 mL/kg IV crystalloids for hypotension or lactate ≥4 mmol/L

Fluid Selection:
  • First-line: Balanced crystalloids (Lactated Ringer's or Plasma-Lyte) preferred over normal saline
  • Alternative: 0.9% Normal Saline (may cause hyperchloremic acidosis with large volumes)
  • Albumin: Consider 5% albumin in addition to crystalloids for patients requiring substantial resuscitation
  • Avoid: Starches (associated with increased AKI and mortality)
  • Reassess frequently: Stop fluids when adequate perfusion achieved to avoid fluid overload

Vasopressor Management

First-Line: Norepinephrine

Dose: 0.05-2 mcg/kg/min IV (typical starting 5-10 mcg/min)

Target: Mean Arterial Pressure (MAP) ≥65 mmHg

Mechanism: α1 > β1 agonist (vasoconstriction + inotropy)

Monitoring: Arterial line for continuous BP, lactate clearance, urine output

Second-Line Options

Vasopressin: 0.03-0.04 units/min (fixed dose, add to norepinephrine)

Epinephrine: 0.05-2 mcg/kg/min (if refractory, may increase lactate)

Phenylephrine: Pure α-agonist, use if tachyarrhythmia limits other agents

Adjunctive Therapy - Hydrocortisone: Consider stress-dose steroids (hydrocortisone 50mg IV q6h or 200mg/day continuous infusion) if shock is refractory to fluids and vasopressors. Controversial benefit per ADRENAL and APROCCHSS trials.

Source Control Urgency Assessment

Timing of source control intervention impacts mortality. Categorize urgency:

Category 1: IMMEDIATE (<6 hours)

High mortality without emergent intervention

  • Necrotizing soft tissue infection (fasciitis, myonecrosis)
  • Perforated viscus with peritonitis
  • Intestinal ischemia/infarction
  • Ascending cholangitis with biliary obstruction

Action: URGENT surgical/interventional consultation

Category 2: URGENT (6-24 hours)

Timely intervention improves outcomes

  • Intra-abdominal abscess requiring drainage
  • Acute cholecystitis
  • Obstructive pyelonephritis (infected hydronephrosis)
  • Empyema requiring drainage
  • Device-related infection (central line, prosthetic)

Action: Early surgical/IR consultation, plan intervention within 12-24h

Category 3: ELECTIVE/PLANNED (After Stabilization)

Stabilize medically first, then plan definitive management

  • Stable abscess amenable to antibiotics alone initially
  • Infected necrotic tissue requiring demarcation (e.g., necrotizing pancreatitis)
  • Osteomyelitis (chronic)

Action: Optimize with antibiotics, consider delayed definitive procedure

Common Post-Sepsis Complications

Physical Impairments (40-80%)

  • Muscle weakness and fatigue
  • Reduced exercise tolerance
  • Joint pain and mobility limitations
  • Difficulty with ADLs

Cognitive Dysfunction (30-60%)

  • Memory impairment
  • Attention and concentration deficits
  • Executive function problems
  • Comparable to mild-moderate TBI

Psychological (30-50%)

  • PTSD symptoms
  • Depression
  • Anxiety
  • Sleep disturbances

Organ-Specific (Variable)

  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Restrictive lung disease
  • Cardiac dysfunction
  • Increased cardiovascular events
